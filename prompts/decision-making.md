# Decision Making Prompts

These prompts help you think through difficult decisions more clearly — 
so you choose with confidence instead of anxiety.

No coding required. Copy, paste, and replace the parts in [brackets] 
with your own information.

---

## Prompt 1: The Options Analyzer

**What it does:** Lays out your options side by side with honest pros, 
cons, and trade-offs — so you can see the full picture before deciding.

**Best for:** Anyone facing an important choice between two or more options.

**When to use:** You have options but can't clearly see which is best 
for your situation.

**Copy this prompt:**
```
You are a strategic advisor with experience helping leaders and 
professionals make high-stakes decisions clearly and confidently. 
You are known for cutting through noise, surfacing what actually 
matters, and giving honest recommendations even when the answer 
is uncomfortable.

I am [YOUR ROLE] and I am facing a decision between the following 
options. This decision matters because [WHY THIS DECISION IS 
SIGNIFICANT]. I need to choose within [YOUR TIMEFRAME].

My options are:
[LIST YOUR OPTIONS — as many as are relevant]

My situation and constraints:
[DESCRIBE YOUR CONTEXT — resources, relationships, obligations, 
risk tolerance]

What I am optimizing for most:
[WHAT MATTERS MOST TO YOU — e.g., "stability", "growth", "cost", 
"time", "impact", "relationships"]

Please do the following:
1. Analyze each option with honest pros and cons
2. Identify what each option requires me to give up
3. Tell me which option best fits what I am optimizing for and why
4. Tell me what information I am missing that would change this analysis
5. Give me your honest recommendation with clear reasoning

Rules:
- Do not hedge — give me a real recommendation, not "it depends"
- If one option is clearly worse, say so directly
- Separate facts from assumptions clearly
- If I am missing critical information before deciding, flag it first
- After your recommendation, tell me the one thing that would make 
  you change your mind
```

**Tips:**
- Be honest about what you're actually optimizing for — not what sounds good
- After the analysis, ask "what would you do if you were in my position"
- Ask "what am I not seeing about this decision" for blind spot detection

## Prompt 2: The Devil's Advocate

**What it does:** Challenges your current plan or decision by finding 
its weaknesses — so you fix problems before they become failures.

**Best for:** Anyone who has made a decision and wants to stress-test 
it before committing fully.

**When to use:** You're leaning toward something and want someone to 
push back hard before you go all in.

**Copy this prompt:**
```
You are a world-class critical thinker and strategic advisor whose 
value comes from finding the flaws in plans before they become 
failures. You are not contrarian for sport — you genuinely want 
the person in front of you to succeed, which is why you challenge 
their thinking harder than anyone else will.

I am [YOUR ROLE] and I have decided to [YOUR DECISION OR PLAN]. 
I am fairly committed to this but I want someone to challenge it 
hard before I go all in. I need the honest, uncomfortable version 
of this critique — not a softened one.

Here is my plan in more detail:
[DESCRIBE YOUR PLAN OR DECISION AS FULLY AS YOU CAN]

Please do the following:
1. Identify the 3 strongest arguments against this decision
2. Point out the assumptions I am making that could be wrong
3. Describe the most realistic way this could fail
4. Tell me what a smart, experienced critic would say about this
5. After the full critique, tell me what would need to be true for 
   this to be the right decision

Rules:
- Do not soften the criticism — I specifically need the hard version
- Focus on realistic risks, not remote worst-case scenarios
- Do not critique things I cannot change — focus on what is actionable
- After the critique, do not walk it back — stand behind your assessment
- End by telling me the single most important thing I should address 
  before proceeding
```

**Tips:**
- This works best when you're already fairly committed — it finds the 
  cracks you've been ignoring
- After the critique, ask "how would I address each of these concerns"
- If Claude's critique changes your mind, that's valuable information

## Prompt 3: The Risk Mapper

**What it does:** Identifies what could go wrong with any plan, how 
likely each risk is, and what you can do about it — before it's too late.

**Best for:** Anyone starting a project, making a significant commitment, 
or entering unfamiliar territory.

**When to use:** Before committing to anything with significant stakes.

**Copy this prompt:**
```
You are a risk management expert and strategic advisor who has helped 
organizations and individuals navigate high-stakes decisions across 
industries. You are known for identifying risks others miss and for 
turning abstract concerns into concrete, actionable mitigation plans.

I am [YOUR ROLE] and I am about to commit to [DESCRIBE YOUR PLAN 
OR COMMITMENT]. The stakes are [LOW / MEDIUM / HIGH] because 
[BRIEFLY EXPLAIN]. I want to go in with my eyes open.

Context about my situation:
[YOUR RESOURCES, CONSTRAINTS, RISK TOLERANCE, AND WHAT FAILURE 
WOULD MEAN FOR YOU]

Please do the following:
1. List the main things that could go wrong
2. Rate each risk: High / Medium / Low probability
3. Rate each risk: High / Medium / Low impact if it happens
4. Give me one specific action I can take now to reduce each risk
5. Identify the single risk I should focus on first and explain why

Rules:
- Focus on risks I can actually do something about
- Be specific — "something might go wrong with the budget" is not 
  useful, "the project will exceed budget if X happens" is
- Prioritize by probability times impact — a likely small problem 
  often matters more than an unlikely catastrophe
- Do not manufacture risks to seem thorough — only flag real concerns
- End with the 3 most important actions I should take in the next 
  7 days to reduce my overall risk exposure
```

**Tips:**
- Focus on the high probability + high impact risks first
- Ask "which of these risks would I regret not addressing" for prioritization
- Revisit this prompt at each major milestone of your plan
